## **What is a committee?**

A Committee is a group of five to eight council members that focus on specific areas like the environment, transportation, or housing. Committees review recommendations from city staff, ask questions, and suggest changes. These recommendations are formed at committee meetings, publicly accessible events which take place at City Hall. A schedule of upcoming committee meetings can be found [here](https://secure.toronto.ca/council/#/highlights).

## **What do committees do?**

Committees are in effect a workshop for laws and policies the City is considering implementing. The Councillors on a committee are tasked with reviewing and critiquing policy recommendations made by City staff. They are expected to integrate feedback from the public into these critiques. It is important to understand that committees do not pass laws. The end goal of a committee is to determine whether the policy recommendation under review needs to be refined by City staff or can be sent to the full City Council for voting.

## **How are committees formed?**

The Mayor holds the power to create committees, but the responsibility for appointing councillors to them is a joint effort between the full City Council and a special committee known as the [Striking Committee](https://secure.toronto.ca/council/#/committees/2463/27872). Councillors put their names forward to the Striking Committee, which subsequently decides who to recommend to the full City Council as official committee members.

## **Committee Meetings**

Meetings are where committee members consider policy reports and recommendations from City staff, provide feedback, suggest changes and ensure agenda items are refined before being sent to the full Council for voting.

Committee meetings are the main arena in which the public can engage with new laws and policies before they are voted on by City Council. Anyone can attend committee meetings in person, submit a written comment on a Committee’s agenda items or request to speak at the meeting, known as making a deputation, to directly communicate their perspective on the issues before the Committee. Meetings are streamed on the Toronto City Council’s [YouTube channel](https://www.youtube.com/TorontoCityCouncilLive), and you can learn more about making deputations via our deputations wiki. 

Occasionally, meetings are closed to the public when they involve discussions containing confidential, sensitive or privileged information. For example, a meeting may be closed if it deals with:

* Security issues with City property  
* Personal matters about City employees  
* Labour relations and negotiations  
* Trade secrets which could affect the competitive position of an organization  
* Trade secrets belonging to the City which possess monetary value  
* Education or training of committee members

## **Types of Committees**

Committees can be broadly sorted into three categories: **Standing Committees**, **Community Councils** and **Ad Hoc Committees**. 

**Standing Committees** consist of four permanent, ongoing committees created by City Council to oversee specific broad policy areas:

* [**Economic and Community Development Committee**](https://secure.toronto.ca/council/#/committees/2563/25831)**:** Takes up recommendations related to employment, equity and reconciliation, food banks and shelters.

* [**General Government and Licensing Committee**](https://secure.toronto.ca/council/#/committees/2542/25823)**:** Awards purchases and contracts to private companies, oversees major revitalization projects,  
    
* [**Infrastructure and Environment Committee**](https://secure.toronto.ca/council/#/committees/2566/25844)**:** Handles issues involving the City’s green space, cycling infrastructure and policies aimed at enhancing Toronto’s environmental sustainability. 

* [**Planning and Housing Committee**](https://secure.toronto.ca/council/#/committees/2565/25815)**:** Addresses housing development, rental standards, transportation infrastructure, cooperatives. 

**Community Councils** oversee local issues within specific areas of the city. They each cover multiple wards and form their membership from local councillors. They are the only committees which can take action and implement changes without going to City Council. 

The four Community Councils are:

* [Etobicoke York Community Council](https://secure.toronto.ca/council/#/committees/2464/25866)  
* [North York Community Council](https://secure.toronto.ca/council/#/committees/2465/25841)  
* [Scarborough Community Council](https://secure.toronto.ca/council/#/committees/2467/25797)  
* [Toronto and East York Community Council](https://secure.toronto.ca/council/#/committees/2466/25854)

A map of the specific area council oversees can be found [here](https://www.toronto.ca/city-government/data-research-maps/neighbourhoods-communities/community-council-area-profiles/).

Lastly, **Ad Hoc Committees** (or **Special Committees**) are committees established by City Council with a specialized goal. These committees can be temporary, as they are assigned to tackle a specific project and disperse once they have seen things through. For example, the [FIFA World Cup 2026 Subcommittee](https://secure.toronto.ca/council/#/committees/2904/26970), which was formed to make recommendations to Council matters related to the World Cup. But, Ad Hoc Committees can also be recurring or permanent, such as the [Audit Committee](https://secure.toronto.ca/council/#/committees/2582/26078) or [Budget Committee](https://secure.toronto.ca/council/#/committees/2562/25834).

A list of all current City Council Committees can be found [here](https://secure.toronto.ca/council/#/committees).
